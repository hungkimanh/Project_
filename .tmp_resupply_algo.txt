Coordinated Truck Routing and Multi-Rendezvous
Drone Resupply under Shipment Release Dates

3

Abstract

4

Efficient coordination between trucks and drones is a critical challenge in dis-

5

tribution systems with shipment release dates, where delayed order availability

6

can disrupt planned routes. In such settings, drones can be used as resupply

7

assets to replenish trucks en route, improving route continuity and fleet effi-

8

ciency. Existing drone-truck routing models under release-date constraints

9

typically restrict each drone flight to a single truck rendezvous, limiting syn-

10

chronization opportunities and constraining potential improvements in system

11

performance. This paper addresses this limitation by studying a coordinated

12

truck-drone routing framework in which a single drone flight can resupply

13

multiple trucks sequentially. The objective is to jointly optimize truck routes

14

and drone schedules to minimize total system completion time while enabling

15

tighter coordination between ground and aerial resources. We formulate the

16

problem as a mixed-integer linear program and develop a tailored Adaptive

17

Tabu Search that integrates truck routing and drone scheduling decisions

18

within a unified optimization framework. Computational experiments demon-

19

strate that the proposed approach outperforms conventional single-rendezvous

20

resupply strategies and reproduces or improves upon MILP-based solutions

21

with substantially reduced computation times. Extensions to related drone-

22

resupply variants with release dates, together with extensive tests on 162

23

benchmark instances including 55 new best-known solutions, further confirm

24

the robustness of the approach and its practical relevance for organizations

25

managing coordinated truck routing and drone resupply operations.

26

Keywords: multi-rendezvous; release date; drone resupply; routing; tabu

1. Introduction

29

The coordination of trucks and unmanned aerial vehicles (UAVs), com-

30

monly referred to as drones, has attracted increasing attention in last-mile

31

logistics due to its potential to improve delivery efficiency and system respon-

32

siveness [1]. A large body of literature has focused on direct-to-customer

33

drone delivery, motivated in part by industrial initiatives such as Amazon

34

Prime Air and UPS Flight Forward.

35

Despite this growing interest, in many real-world logistics systems the final

36

handover of parcels must still be performed by a human operator. Operational

37

requirements such as identity verification, proof-of-delivery procedures, secure

38

handling of regulated goods, or cash-on-delivery transactions often require

39

direct interaction between the customer and a truck driver. Under such

40

conditions, drones may reach the delivery location but cannot independently

41

complete the customer service process.

42

In these environments, drones can instead be deployed as resupply assets

43

supporting trucks during route execution. Rather than delivering parcels

44

directly to customers, drones transport newly available packages from a depot

45

to trucks already operating in the field. Several recent studies have explored

46

such drone-assisted resupply mechanisms in logistics systems, highlighting
2

47

their potential to improve operational flexibility and system responsiveness

48

[2, 3, 4, 5]. Motivated by these operational contexts, this study focuses on a

49

pure resupply mode, in which drones interact exclusively with trucks while

50

all customer deliveries remain truck-based.

51

In truck–drone resupply systems, synchronization between drones and

52

trucks is typically achieved through rendezvous operations along truck routes.

53

A key characteristic of the problem studied in this paper is the allowance

54

of multi-rendezvous drone sorties, whereby a single drone may sequentially

55

rendezvous with multiple trucks during one flight. In contrast, most existing

56

truck–drone resupply studies adopt a single-rendezvous assumption, restricting

57

each drone sortie to at most one interaction with a truck. This assumption

58

simplifies synchronization and maintains model tractability. However, when

59

drones possess sufficient endurance and payload capacity, limiting each sortie

60

to a single rendezvous can lead to underutilization of drone resources, increased

61

truck waiting times, and fragmented resupply operations, particularly in

62

environments where truck routes are spatially clustered or customer orders

63

arrive in batches. Figure 1 illustrates such an operational setting, where trucks

64

execute customer deliveries along predefined routes while a drone performs

65

one or multiple rendezvous to resupply different trucks during a single sortie.

66

Another important operational aspect concerns the availability of parcels

67

over time. In many logistics systems, particularly in large-scale e-commerce

68

fulfillment operations, orders become available gradually due to order cut-

69

off times, sorting delays, or consolidation processes at distribution centers.

70

Release-date constraints have therefore been considered in a limited number

71

of truck-drone resupply studies to represent such batch order arrivals [4, 5].

72

As trucks continue their delivery routes while new parcels become avail-

73

able, drones may naturally rendezvous with multiple trucks sequentially to

74

distribute these parcels efficiently. Building on this modeling perspective,

75

we consider release dates in a multi-rendezvous resupply setting, in which

76

a drone may carry parcels with different availability times and sequentially

77

rendezvous with multiple trucks as these parcels are released. This combina-

78

tion of release dates and multi-rendezvous resupply creates stronger temporal

79

interdependencies between truck routing and drone scheduling decisions than

80

in traditional single-rendezvous formulations.

81

From a methodological perspective, static truck-drone resupply problems

82

with release dates have so far been addressed mainly through hierarchical

83

coordination schemes. Existing studies typically either fix truck routes and

84

construct the drone resupply plan once by solving a mixed-integer linear

85

program (MILP), or embed truck-route local search within an iterative frame4

86

work in which the drone resupply schedule is reconstructed from scratch via

87

MILP for each modified truck solution. While the latter approach captures

88

tighter coordination, repeatedly rebuilding drone schedules incurs substantial

89

computational overhead and may disrupt temporal consistency, particularly in

90

multi-rendezvous settings where routing and scheduling decisions are strongly

91

coupled.

92

To address these limitations, we propose an integrated optimization ap-

93

proach that maintains persistent coordination between truck routing and

94

drone scheduling by incrementally adapting drone resupply plans to truck-

95

route modifications, rather than reconstructing the entire drone schedule at

96

each iteration. Accordingly, this paper investigates the modeling and algorith-

97

mic implications of extending existing truck–drone resupply formulations to a

98

static multi-rendezvous setting with release dates, which provides a controlled

99

benchmark environment to analyze the coordination mechanisms between

100

truck routing and drone resupply operations.

101

The main contributions of this study are threefold. First, we formalize the

102

Vehicle Routing Problem with Release Dates and Multi-Rendezvous Drone

103

Resupply (VRP-MRDR) and present a MILP formulation that captures

104

multi-rendezvous drone sorties capable of sequentially resupplying multiple

105

trucks within a single flight. Second, we develop an Adaptive Tabu Search

106

(ATS) framework that optimizes truck routing and drone scheduling by incre-

107

mentally adapting drone resupply plans to truck-route modifications. Third,

108

computational experiments demonstrate the benefits of multi-rendezvous

109

drone resupply and show that the proposed approach consistently outper-

110

forms conventional two-phase MILP-based methods in both solution quality
5

111

and computational efficiency.

112

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section 2 reviews

113

related literature on truck-drone coordination in last-mile logistics. Section 3

114

describes the problem and presents the mathematical formulation. Section 4

115

introduces the proposed Adaptive Tabu Search algorithm. Section 5 reports

116

computational results, followed by managerial implications in Section 6.

117

Section 7 concludes the paper and outlines future research directions.

2.1. Customer-oriented drone delivery in last-mile logistics
The integration of drones into last-mile delivery operations has attracted

130

considerable research interest as a means to improve delivery efficiency, re-

131

sponsiveness, and sustainability. Comprehensive surveys of drone-assisted

132

last-mile delivery can be found in [6, 7, 8, 9]. Early studies primarily focused

133

on using drones for direct customer delivery, either operating independently

134

or in coordination with ground vehicles. Murray and Chu [10] established a

135

foundational classification by distinguishing between a parallel mode, in which

136

trucks and drones depart from a depot and operate independently, and a

137

synchronization mode, in which trucks serve as mobile platforms for launching

138

and retrieving drones during route execution.

139

Within the parallel mode, numerous studies have investigated single-truck-

140

multi-drone systems departing from a depot, with objectives typically related

141

to minimizing total completion time or operational costs [11, 12, 13, 14].

142

Subsequent research extended this paradigm to more realistic large-scale

143

settings involving multiple trucks and multiple drones [15, 16, 17, 18, 19, 20],

144

addressing scalability and operational complexity.

145

Beyond purely parallel operations, a substantial stream of research has

146

examined synchronization-based truck-drone coordination, in which trucks

147

act as mobile platforms that launch, recover, and support drones along their

148

routes. These studies involve tightly coupled routing and scheduling decisions

149

and include investigations of single truck-single drone systems [21, 22, 23],

150

single truck-multiple drone configurations [24, 25, 26], and multiple truck-

151

multiple drone architectures [27, 28, 29]. More recently, this research stream

152

has been extended to heterogeneous ground vehicle fleets, such as systems
7

153

combining trucks, tricycles, and drones [30].

154

Beyond commercial last-mile delivery, drone-truck coordination has also

155

been investigated in humanitarian logistics, where the objective structure

156

differs from commercial settings. In this domain, operational objectives

157

typically emphasize timely service and reduced waiting times at demand

158

points, while strategic objectives account for fleet composition and capital

159

investment decisions. Relief distribution is commonly modeled as a two-

160

echelon network, in which trucks operate in the first echelon to transport

161

supplies from distribution centers to intermediate nodes, while drones are

162

deployed in the second echelon to serve hard-to-access demand points [31].

163

From a logistics and supply chain perspective, a common characteristic of

164

the above delivery-oriented models, across both commercial and humanitarian

165

settings, is that drones are primarily employed to directly serve customers. As

166

a result, these studies typically assume that all customer orders are available

167

prior to route execution, and inventory availability or order release timing

168

does not play a central role in the routing decisions.

169

2.2. Release-date-based drone resupply

170

In contrast to delivery-oriented models, drone resupply represents an

171

inventory-aware logistics paradigm in which trucks may depart from the

172

depot before all customer orders become available. In this setting, drones

173

are employed to transport newly released packages to trucks while they are

174

en route, thereby coupling routing decisions with order release dates, vehicle

175

capacity constraints, and replenishment timing. This paradigm is particularly

176

relevant in time-critical and same-day delivery contexts, where delayed order

177

availability can significantly affect system performance.
8

178

The concept of drone resupply was first introduced by Dayarian et al. [2],

179

who studied a system comprising a single truck and a single drone under

180

dynamic order arrivals. The drone transports newly arrived packages from

181

the depot to the truck, which then dynamically updates its route. Their

182

objective was to maximize the number of customers served within a given

183

planning horizon, and the benefits of drone resupply were demonstrated using

184

heuristic methods. Building on this work, Pina-Pardo et al. [3] extended the

185

model to include multiple drones supporting a single truck under dynamic

186

demand conditions.

187

Static variants of the drone resupply problem, which are directly relevant

188

to our study, assume that customer locations and order release dates are

189

known in advance, although some orders may not be ready for dispatch at

190

the beginning of operations. Pina-Pardo et al. [4] introduced a model with a

191

single truck and a single drone, aiming to minimize total delivery completion

192

time. The problem was decomposed into a truck routing subproblem and a

193

drone resupply subproblem defined along a predetermined truck route. This

194

framework was later extended to multiple trucks and multiple drones [5],

195

where trucks may either return to the depot or be resupplied by drones during

196

their routes. Truck routing is optimized using tabu search, while a MILP

197

model determines optimal loading actions.

198

Several related studies have examined alternative drone resupply settings

199

under different operational assumptions. Dienstknecht et al. [32] considered

200

a single truck–single drone system with limited truck capacity, while Liu et

201

al. [33] studied resupply operations along fixed truck routes with the objective

202

of maximizing delivered value under release-time constraints. Barzanjeh et
9

203

al. [34] investigated a supporting drone delivery variant within a parallel

204

truck–drone framework with order release times and uncertain drone travel

205

durations. Mohammad et al. [35] proposed a two-echelon system where drones

206

deliver packages to transshipment points rather than directly resupplying

207

trucks, while Sanchez-Wells et al. [36] examined a resupply setting involving

208

a mother truck and a dedicated resupply truck with limited synchronization

209

opportunities.

210

211

related studies, Table 1 summarizes the main characteristics of existing truck-

212

drone resupply models. Although most studies consider drones as the resupply

213

vehicle, Table 1 also includes a related study in which resupply is performed

214

Within the literature on static truck-drone resupply problems with release

216

dates, coordination between truck routing and drone operations has so far

217

been addressed using decomposition-based schemes. Existing studies follow
10

218

two representative approaches. One constructs the drone resupply plan only

219

once along a predetermined truck solution by solving a MILP (e.g., [4]). The

220

other embeds truck-route local search within an iterative framework, in which

221

a subordinate MILP is repeatedly solved to rebuild the resupply or loading

222

plan for each modified truck solution (e.g., [5]). Although the latter approach

223

enables tighter coordination than one-shot constructions, the repeated recon-

224

struction of resupply plans introduces substantial computational overhead

225

and limits scalability, especially when richer synchronization mechanisms are

226

considered.

227

To preserve tractability and simplify synchronization constraints, existing

228

static resupply models almost exclusively restrict each drone sortie to a single

229

rendezvous with a truck. This modeling choice avoids complex temporal

230

dependencies across multiple truck routes and bounds the size of the feasible

231

synchronization space, which becomes particularly challenging when routing,

232

capacity, and release-date constraints are jointly considered. However, when

233

multiple trucks operate simultaneously and parcels become available gradually

234

over time, restricting each sortie to a single rendezvous may lead to inefficient

235

drone utilization and fragmented resupply operations. In such settings,

236

allowing a drone to sequentially rendezvous with multiple trucks within a

237

single sortie can improve coordination efficiency and better exploit available

238

drone endurance and capacity.

239

Within this context, the proposed VRP-MRDR extends existing static

240

truck-drone resupply models by relaxing the commonly adopted single-

241

rendezvous assumption and allowing drones to sequentially rendezvous with

242

multiple trucks within a single sortie. This extension enables the investigation
11

243

of richer synchronization structures between trucks and drones under release-

244

date constraints while maintaining a static demand setting that isolates the

245

coordination mechanisms of interest.

3. Problem description and Mathematical model

247

3.1. Problem description

248

The system in the VRP-MRDR incorporates multiple delivery trucks k ∈ K

249

and resupply drones d ∈ D working in tandem, with all vehicles initially

250

based at a central depot. We assume that trucks are uncapacitated, whereas

251

drones are constrained by both flight endurance Le and payload capacity A.

252

For each customer i ∈ C, the system accounts for a predefined location and

253

order specifications, including a volume ai to be delivered and a release time

254

wi , indicating when the order becomes available at the depot. All parameters

255

are deterministically known at the time of planning. The system initialization

256

occurs at time t = 0. However, trucks are not obligated to depart from the

257

depot at this initial time point. They may delay departure to accommodate

258

orders with later release times. Notably, each truck performs exactly one

259

trip, necessitating a drone-based resupply mechanism for delivering orders

260

that become available after truck departure. The objective is to minimize the

261

overall completion time, which is defined as the time at which all trucks and

262

drones return to the depot after all orders have been delivered. The route

263

planning thus involves two interconnected parts:

264

1. Each truck k ∈ K executes a single trip originating from and terminating

265

at the depot, serving a predetermined sequence of customers. A truck

266

may depart carrying either its complete allocation of assigned packages
12

267

or a subset thereof, contingent upon order availability at departure

268

time.

269

2. Each drone d ∈ D performs one or a series of trips, each originating

270

from the depot to rendezvous with one or multiple trucks at customer

271

locations, ultimately concluding at the depot. Each trip is constrained by

272

the maximum flight time Le , a limitation imposed by battery capacity

273

constraint, and the aggregate mass of transported orders must not

274

exceed the maximum payload threshold A. The drones’ trips facilitate

275

the delivery of orders with release times that postdate the trucks’

276

initial departures. It takes δ time units for a truck receiving the drone,

277

unloading the new orders and launching the drone.

278

The system operates under the following assumptions. Upon arrival at a

279

customer location, if the corresponding order is already on board, the truck

280

delivers the item immediately without any waiting time. Drone–truck ren-

281

dezvous are permitted only at customer locations when the truck is present,

282

and intermediate or arbitrary meeting points are not allowed. Each drone

283

departs from the depot with a fully recharged battery, with depot charging

284

time considered negligible, and remains in continuous flight throughout its

285

sortie. Temporary drone landings to conserve energy while waiting are not

286

permitted, as this would require all customer locations to be safe and autho-

287

rized landing sites, which is not generally valid in practice. This assumption

288

avoids reliance on additional landing infrastructure and yields a conservative

289

and broadly applicable formulation.

13

290

3.2. Mathematical model

291

The VRP-MRDR is defined on a graph G = (V, E) with the vertex set

292

V = {0, 1, .., n} represents the depot (node 0) and the set of customers

293

C = {1, 2, ..., n}, and the edge set E = {(i, j) : i, j ∈ V}. Although a single

294

depot location exists, notationally, we assign it to two unique node numbers,

295

such that trucks/drones depart from the depot at node 0 and return to the

296

depot at node n + 1.

4. Solution methodology

420

To address release-date drone-resupply variants, existing methods rely

421

predominantly on decomposition procedures that optimize truck routes first

422

and drone routes second. This sequential structure reflects the difficulty

423

of constructing both route types simultaneously, as the synchronization

424

requirements between trucks and drones cause the two sets of decisions to be

425

strongly interdependent.

426

The VRP-MRDR intensifies these challenges by allowing multiple ren-

427

dezvous within a single drone sortie, thereby introducing additional coordi-

428

nation requirements. These features increase the algorithmic burden, as the

429

solution process must accommodate more resupply adjustments and maintain
25

430

tighter alignment of waiting times across vehicles. Developing a framework ca-

431

pable of handling these interactions while remaining computationally tractable

432

is therefore nontrivial. In this study, we propose an Adaptive Tabu Search

433

(ATS) algorithm designed to address the VRP-MRDR. The method incorpo-

434

rates multiple neighborhood structures together with adaptive memory and

435

weighting mechanisms, enabling systematic exploration of the solution space

436

while reducing the likelihood of premature convergence.

437

This section outlines the design and components of the proposed ATS

438

algorithm. The solution representation is introduced in Section 4.2, followed

439

by the description of the initial solution construction procedure in Section 4.3.

440

Sections 4.4, 4.5, and 4.6 describe in detail the neighborhood structures, the

441

neighborhood selection strategy, and the tabu status management, respectively.

442

Finally, Sections 4.7 and 4.8 present the embedded local search procedure and

443

the diversification mechanism used to enhance the algorithm’s exploitation

444

and exploration capability.

4.3. Initial solution

499

To rapidly generate an initial feasible solution, we construct a truck-only

500

routing plan by assigning all packages to trucks. Customers are sorted by

501

ascending release times, with the first |K| customers initializing the |K| truck

502

routes. The remaining customers are sequentially inserted into the route that

503

minimizes the system makespan, yielding a feasible baseline solution.

504

This solution is then refined through a two-phase procedure. In the first

505

phase, truck routes are optimized without drone resupply while accounting

506

for package release times via a modified working-time calculation. If a truck

507

serves any customer outside the drone’s operational range, it departs at the

508

latest release time among those customers; otherwise, it departs at time zero.

509

For a customer c reached at time t, if wc > 0 and c is within drone range, the

510

D
departure time is max{t, wc + τ0c
}; otherwise, arrival and departure times

511

coincide. A tabu search is then applied using four neighborhood structures,

512

(1, 0), (1, 1), (2, 1), and 2-opt, randomly selected at each iteration, with the

513

search limited to |C| iterations to give each customer a chance to change its

514

position while ensuring reasonable computation time.

515

In the second phase, a local search procedure constructs and refines drone

516

resupply trips based on the Phase 1 truck routes, with all packages initially

517

located at the depot and no predefined drone trips. The same set of three

518

operators described in Section 4.7 is employed.
30

519

In this construction stage, the package relocation operator plays a crucial

520

role, as it relocates packages from their current loading or transfer positions

521

along a truck route to alternative feasible locations, thereby enabling the

522

creation of initial drone trips when no synchronization points yet exist. Once

523

drone trips have been established, all operators may further modify the current

524

drone schedule, enabling consolidation, splitting, or resequencing of trips to

525

improve synchronization.

526

By confining the modified truck working-time calculation to Phase 1,

527

trucks are scheduled to load all ready packages from the depot, while non-

528

ready packages are handled through drone resupply. This separation allows

529

Phase 2 to focus primarily on drone assignment and synchronization decisions

530

without repeated truck route re-optimization, leading to faster convergence

531

toward high-quality solutions that respect package availability and maintain

532

effective truck-drone synchronization.

533

4.4. Neighborhoods

534

The proposed algorithm is organized into two sequential components

535

with distinct roles. The first component performs a neighborhood search

536

that focuses exclusively on modifying truck routes. A set N of truck-based

537

neighborhood moves, including (1,0), (1,1), 2-opt, and (2,1), as described in

538

[16], is applied to change the assignment and visiting order of customers on

539

trucks. At this stage, drone feasibility and synchronization constraints are

540

deliberately ignored, allowing the search to explore promising truck-route

541

structures without being overly restricted by drone-related considerations.

542

Once a truck-route modification is generated, the second component is

543

invoked as a deterministic repair and synchronization procedure to restore
31

544

feasibility by adjusting drone operations, aiming to preserve existing schedules

545

while limiting additional computational effort. For each affected customer,

546

packages are reassigned sequentially along the updated truck route using

547

a hierarchical strategy. Direct truck assignment is first attempted if the

548

package’s release time does not exceed the maximum release time among

549

the packages currently loaded onto the truck at the depot. Otherwise, an

550

existing drone synchronization point along the same route is reused, subject

551

to drone capacity and temporal constraints. If neither option is feasible, a

552

new drone trip is created. Since such a trip can always be introduced within

553

the operational constraints of the system, the repair mechanism guarantees

554

that feasibility is preserved.

555

572

Separate tabu lists are maintained for each move category, with tabu

573

574

Consequently, θ is randomly selected from the interval [2 · ln |C|, |C|].

4.7. Local search

582

The synchronization between drones and trucks in the VRP-MRDR creates

583

a strong interdependency, whereby modifications to either vehicle’s route

584

directly affect the system completion time, which is the primary optimization

585

objective. Since the tabu search neighborhoods mainly modify truck routes,
33

586

corresponding adjustments to drone trips are required. To further improve

587

solution quality, dedicated local search neighborhoods are introduced to modify

588

drone trip patterns and reassign trips between drones with the objective of

589

reducing waiting times at synchronization points, thereby improving temporal

590

coordination and decreasing overall completion time. The local search employs

591

three neighborhood operators: synchronization point relocation, package

592

relocation, and drone trip reordering. The synchronization point relocation

593

operator moves a synchronization point from one drone trip to another,

594

selecting the insertion position that minimizes additional travel time, subject

595

to release-time feasibility to limit computational effort. The package relocation

596

operator transfers one or more packages to alternative feasible locations along

597

the corresponding truck route, consolidating them at existing synchronization

598

points when possible or creating a new drone trip otherwise. The drone trip

599

reordering operator explores alternative execution sequences by repositioning

600

a drone trip within the drone queue.

601

The local search sequentially applies all three operators, evaluating moves

602

on all truck and drone routes and stopping at the first improvement. The

603

best improving solution among the three neighborhoods is accepted, and the

604

process repeats until no further improvement is found.

605

4.8. Diversification

606

To mitigate the risk of the algorithm becoming trapped in local optima, a

607

diversification phase is introduced. This phase is activated after SEG consec-

608

utive segments during which no improvement is observed in the current best

609

solution pbest . The diversification strategy employs a perturbation mechanism

610

based on subsequence reversal within each truck route of the current solution
34

611

p. Specifically, for each truck route, the algorithm randomly selects an integer

612

r in the range [2, ⌊x/2⌋], where x is the number of customers served by that

613

truck. It then systematically evaluates all subsequences within the truck route

614

of length ranging from r up to ⌊x/2⌋, applying a reversal operation to each.

615

These operations generate a set of perturbed candidate solutions.

616

After each subsequence reversal, the resulting solution is passed through

617

the same deterministic repair and synchronization procedure used in the

618

neighborhood search phase to restore drone feasibility. In particular, drone

619

trips affected by the modified truck route are adjusted to ensure temporal

620

consistency and synchronization feasibility. Among the repaired candidates,

621

the best feasible solution is retained for the continuation of the search.

622

This diversification mechanism balances the preservation of favorable

623

route structures with the introduction of sufficient variation to escape local

624

optima. By periodically redirecting the search toward unexplored regions of

625

the solution space, it enables the algorithm to identify improved solutions

626

that are unlikely to be reached through standard tabu search iterations alone,

627

thereby enhancing overall solution quality.

5.1. Instance generation and parameter definition

641

The experimental dataset employed in this study is constructed by building

642

on the instance design proposed by Pina-Pardo et al. [4, 5]. Following their

643

framework, the instances include between 10 and 100 customers, with customer

644

locations drawn from the Solomon benchmark sets (C101, C201, R101, and

645

RC101). Customer release times are independently sampled from a uniform

646

distribution over the interval [0, β · dT SP ], where dT SP denotes the optimal

647

tour length of the corresponding traveling salesman problem instance and

648

β ∈ {0.5, 1, 1.5, 2.0, 2.5, 3.0}. Truck travel times are computed using the

649

Manhattan distance metric at an average speed of 30 km/h, while drone

650

travel times are based on Euclidean distances at 60 km/h. Each drone is

651

subject to an endurance limit of 90 minutes and a service cycle time of δ = 5

652

minutes, accounting for retrieval, unloading, and redeployment. Consistent

653

with prior work, the fleet size scales with the number of customers: instances

654

with 10, 15, or 20 customers use one truck and one drone; instances with

655

30 or 50 customers use two trucks and two drones; and instances with 100

656

customers use three trucks and three drones.

657

Unlike Pina-Pardo et al. [4, 5], who assume unit customer demands, we

658

introduce heterogeneous demand levels by assigning each customer i ∈ C a

659

random demand ai ∈ {1, 2, 3, 4, 5}. In our setting, drones are allowed to serve
36

660

only customers whose demand does not exceed the drone payload capacity,

661

thereby reflecting more realistic operational constraints and differentiating

662

our experimental design from existing benchmarks.

663

To summarize, for each of the four instance sets (C101, C201, R101,

664

RC101), 36 instances are generated based on six customer sizes {10, 15, 20,

665

30, 50, 100} and six values of β ∈ {0.5, 1.0, 1.5, 2.0, 2.5, 3.0}, resulting in 144

666

base instances across all sets. By further incorporating three distinct drone

667

payload capacities from the discrete set {2, 4, 8} units, each of the 144 base

668

instances is extended to three variants. This yields a total of 432 instances in

669

our complete dataset.

5.4. Comparison with results in literature

774

This section compares the performance of the proposed ATS with results

775

available in the literature for the drone resupply problems TSPRD-DR and

776

VRPRD-DR. Existing approaches are predominantly two phase decomposition

777

methods that first generate truck routes and then keep them fixed while opti-

778

mizing drone routes. This sequential approach restricts the solution space and

779

may lead to suboptimal outcomes. To overcome these limitations, our ATS dy-

780

namically adjusts both truck and drone routes throughout the search process,

781

providing stronger exploration and exploitation capabilities. This integrated

782

approach allows our ATS to obtain higher quality solutions while maintaining

783

competitive computational efficiency compared with decomposition-based

784

methods.

6. Managerial implications
Our study yields managerial implications for organizations managing coor-

932

dinated truck routing and drone resupply operations under shipment release

933

dates. The results indicate that, within the operational setting considered,

934

drones provide managerial benefits when deployed as truck-support assets

935

in an integrated truck-drone operation, where effective coordination reduces

936

truck idle time and improves route continuity. Fleet configuration decisions

937

should therefore be treated as context dependent: more drone-intensive fleets

938

are preferable in environments with dispersed and uncertain order release

939

times, whereas truck-centric fleets remain effective in stable and predictable
52

940

settings. Moreover, coordination policies that restrict a drone to a single

941

rendezvous per trip can limit operational efficiency; allowing drones to ren-

942

dezvous with multiple trucks within a trip, as in the multi-rendezvous strategy,

943

leads to consistently better makespan performance and greater operational

944

flexibility. Finally, technology investment decisions should recognize drone

945

payload capacity as a system-level lever, since higher-capacity drones enhance

946

operational robustness and reduce sensitivity to demand variability.

947

7. Conclusions

948

This study introduces the VRP-MRDR, which extends existing truck–drone

949

resupply models by allowing a drone to sequentially rendezvous with multiple

950

trucks within a single sortie. We present a MILP formulation and develop

951

an Adaptive Tabu Search algorithm, which is further extended to solve the

952

TSPRD-DR and VRPRD-DR variants. Computational experiments demon-

953

strate that the proposed approach consistently produces high-quality solutions

954

and discovers numerous new best-known results while significantly reducing

955

computation times.

956

The results highlight the operational advantages of multi-rendezvous drone

957

resupply compared with the traditional single-rendezvous setting, particularly

958

under shipment release dates where demand becomes available over time.

959

From a methodological perspective, this study serves as a benchmark analy-

960

sis of static multi-rendezvous truck–drone resupply, providing a controlled

961

environment to examine coordination mechanisms between truck routing and

962

drone resupply.

963

Future research may extend this benchmark framework to more realistic
53

964

logistics environments, such as dynamic order arrivals, hybrid operations

965

where drones also perform direct deliveries, or integrated pickup-and-delivery

966

settings.
